01 DATA STORAGE TIERS COST OPTIMIZATION B2B SAAS shipped

Cutting storage cost 44% across hot, warm, and cold tiers.

Twelve enterprise customers needed a clean tiered-storage strategy — but the platform was charging premium rates on cold data that hadn't been queried in six months. The fix was a customer-research problem first, an engineering problem second.

the problem

Storage tiers were billed flat — no automatic promotion/demotion based on access patterns. Customers paid SSD prices for archival data. The team had built retention policies but never instrumented adoption. SLO targets were defined in abstract; COGS profile didn't match what customers were actually consuming. Pricing felt like guesswork to the sales team.

my approach

40+ primary research interviews with platform engineers and FinOps buyers. Built a researched perspective on storage access patterns — hot reads vs warm queries vs cold archives. Established SLOs per tier. Rebuilt the COGS profile from raw S3 + compute cost data. Reframed pricing around access frequency, not stored volume. Worked cross-functionally with Product, Engineering, Marketing, and Sales to roll out a tiered offering customers could reason about.

02 B2B PLATFORM RELIABILITY AGILE shipped

From 12% errors to 1.4% — one SDK redesign.

A top-5 US bank's commission-payout engine ran at 12% error rates, quietly costing $2M+ a quarter in reconciliation. Six teams, three systems, no shared source of truth. The fix was a platform problem dressed up as a feature problem.

the problem

Payment logic lived across three systems with subtle inconsistencies. Each consuming team had built its own client integration — each handling idempotency and retries differently. The deeper issue: every team was writing the same retry loops in slightly wrong ways. The platform needed an opinionated SDK before it needed more features.

my approach

Four-week discovery sprint. Shadowed 12 commission analysts. Performed primary research interviewing end-users and buyers. Set priorities and shaped the roadmap around three releases. Acted as product owner for the Scrum team, managed the backlog, groomed stories, broke down tasks, drove clarity. Built and operated software as a service with agile processes and tight feedback loops.

03 CUSTOMER RESEARCH UX GTM ENABLEMENT shipped

From customer interview to shipped feature in three weeks.

Twelve enterprise customers had been complaining about onboarding friction for two quarters — but every team interpreted the feedback differently. We needed a research discipline that produced shared truth, not parallel hypotheses.

the problem

Customer feedback was reaching the product team through 5 different channels, each filtered differently — Sales saw it through "they want feature X," Support saw it through tickets, CS saw it through QBR slides. The team had data but no shared interpretation. Nobody could turn customer research into a coherent strategy because there wasn't a coherent customer narrative.

my approach

Built a research cadence — weekly ride-alongs in customer conversations, monthly synthesis sessions, quarterly competitive diligence reviews. Partnered with UX to build delightful features focused on the small details that make products loved and usable. Coordinated with product marketing on positioning and messaging. how i work

Six rules for technical PMs who ship.

01

Storage tiers are a product decision

Hot, warm, cold isn't an engineering detail — it's a pricing decision, a customer-trust decision, a COGS decision. Treat tier policy as roadmap, not infra.

02

Be a peer to engineering

If I can't reason about idempotency, distributed systems, and cardinality budgets in the same room as the engineers, I'm not actually doing technical PM work.

03

Customer research is a craft

Primary research with end-users and buyers, ride-alongs in customer conversations, deeply understanding the competition. Not a checkbox — a weekly discipline.

04

COGS profile tells the truth

If your COGS profile doesn't match what customers actually consume, your pricing model is fiction. I rebuild COGS from raw data, not from accounting summaries.

05

The small details matter

Empty states. Error messages. The first 10 minutes of a developer's first session. These are where products earn trust — or quietly lose it.

06

Ship — then iterate

I'd rather ship a smaller v1 with clear SLOs and a tight feedback loop than a perfect v2 that arrives a quarter late. Four consecutive on-time releases isn't a happy accident.
